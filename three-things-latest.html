Drift Protocol Suffers £216M Hack via Social Engineering, Not Code Exploit

North Korean threat actors extracted £216 million ($285 million) from the Solana-based DeFi platform Drift Protocol using social engineering and a fabricated token (CarbonVote), not a cryptographic vulnerability. The attack, attributed to the Lazarus Group by TRM Labs, exploited human-controlled security gates rather than technical ones, succeeding in under 20 minutes.

Why this matters

Financial institutions evaluating crypto-native infrastructure now face evidence that billion-pound platforms can be breached without exploiting code. Social engineering is the binding constraint on infrastructure security, not cryptography.

2

Only 3% of US Consumer Households Are Paying for AI Products

Bank of America internal data reveals that despite massive adoption of free-tier AI tools, only 3% of US households are paying subscribers to AI services. Gen Z households show 54% year-on-year spending growth, but absolute penetration remains negligible. Over 60% of businesses now allocate 5% or more of tech spending to AI.

Why this matters

The consumer AI revenue model remains unproven at scale. The gap between enterprise AI spending (accelerating) and consumer willingness to pay (stalling) suggests the next battleground is converting free users to paying customers.

3

Bank of England Flags Agentic AI Risk in Payments and Markets

The Bank of England's Financial Policy Committee (April 2026 record) acknowledged that agentic AI has not yet created systemic risk but warned that risks are "likely to increase, potentially rapidly". The FPC requested further work on agentic AI behaviour in payments and financial markets, signalling regulatory attention is accelerating ahead of deployment.

Why this matters

Central bank attention on agentic AI in payments suggests governance frameworks will be imposed before the technology matures. Institutions building agentic payment systems should anticipate pre-emptive regulatory constraints.
